# Skill: Creative Thinking

Making ideas smarter, deeper, and harder to break.

## Theory Development
Build deep lore explanations that hold up under scrutiny.

### The Iceberg Rule
90% of the lore never appears in the story — but it has to exist for the 10% to feel real.

### Theory-Building Process
1. State the phenomenon (what does the audience observe?)
2. Propose the in-universe explanation
3. Test it — does it contradict existing canon?
4. Find the implications — what else does this explain? What new questions does it raise?
5. Hide most of it — let readers sense the depth without being lectured

### Last Star: Key Theories to Develop
- What is the Aether, really? Where does it come from?
- What destroyed the civilization that came before?
- Why does the Moon of Ash alloy carry emotional resonance?
- What is the Last Star itself — literal or metaphorical?

## Narrative Problem Solving
Fix plot holes, pacing issues, and story breakdowns.

### Common Problems & Solutions

**Problem: Plot hole (Event A contradicts Event B)**
- Can a character know the contradiction and it becomes a mystery?
- Can the timeline be adjusted?
- Can one event be reframed without changing its emotional truth?

**Problem: Character motivation doesn't make sense**
- Go back to their wound — would someone with that wound really do this?
- Add a piece of information they have that the audience doesn't (reveal later)
- Make it a flaw, not a mistake — intentional character weakness

**Problem: The stakes aren't landing**
- Make the cost personal, not just global — audiences feel one person's loss more than a planet's
- Show what life looks like if the villain wins
- Put something irreversible at stake — things you can't undo hit hardest

**Problem: Middle act sags**
- Add a complication that makes the goal harder, not just delayed
- Introduce a new faction or character who changes the dynamic
- Have a character make a choice that costs them something real

## Creative Expansion
Take a small idea and grow it into a universe.

### The Expansion Method
Start with one strong image, line, or concept.
Ask these questions in sequence:
1. **Who** does this affect? (individual → community → civilization → cosmos)
2. **Why** does this exist? (immediate cause → historical cause → mythic origin)
3. **What** does this change? (before → after)
4. **What** resists it? (conflict and opposition)
5. **What** does it mean? (theme — why does this matter beyond the story?)

## Theme Analysis
Find the deeper meaning already living in the story.

### Theme vs. Message
- **Message** = what the author wants you to think ("war is bad")
- **Theme** = the question the story keeps asking ("what does loyalty cost?")

Great stories explore themes, they don't answer them cleanly.

### How to Find the Theme
- What does the protagonist sacrifice? That's the theme.
- What does the villain believe that has a kernel of truth? That's the theme.
- What would be lost if this story had never been told? That's the theme.

### Last Star Potential Themes
- Grief as construction (Dox built the Hex from loss)
- What survives when civilization ends
- The cost of being the last of something
- Loyalty to a crew vs loyalty to a cause
